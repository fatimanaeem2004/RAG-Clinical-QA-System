CLINICAL GUIDELINES FOR CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASE AND HYPERTENSION

SECTION 1: DIAGNOSIS AND CLASSIFICATION OF HYPERTENSION

Hypertension is a primary risk factor for cardiovascular disease (CVD), stroke, and renal failure. Accurate measurement and classification are critical for guiding treatment decisions.

1.1 BLOOD PRESSURE MEASUREMENT PROTOCOL
For a proper diagnosis of hypertension, blood pressure (BP) must be measured using validated devices and standardized protocols.
- Patient Preparation: The patient should sit quietly for at least 5 minutes before measurement, with their back supported, feet flat on the floor, and arm supported at heart level. No caffeine, exercise, or smoking should occur for at least 30 minutes prior to measurement.
- Measurement Technique: Use an appropriately sized cuff (bladder width should be at least 40% of arm circumference). Obtain at least two readings on two or more separate occasions to confirm the diagnosis, except in cases of hypertensive crisis or severe target organ damage.

1.2 BLOOD PRESSURE CLASSIFICATION CATEGORIES (AHA/ACC GUIDELINES)
- Normal Blood Pressure: Systolic BP < 120 mmHg and Diastolic BP < 80 mmHg.
- Elevated Blood Pressure: Systolic BP between 120–129 mmHg and Diastolic BP < 80 mmHg. Lifestyle modifications are indicated; pharmacological therapy is not recommended at this stage unless higher risk factors exist.
- Stage 1 Hypertension: Systolic BP between 130–139 mmHg or Diastolic BP between 80–89 mmHg. Treatment is guided by the patient's 10-year atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD) risk score.
- Stage 2 Hypertension: Systolic BP >= 140 mmHg or Diastolic BP >= 90 mmHg. Dual-class pharmacological therapy is generally recommended at this stage.
- Hypertensive Crisis: Systolic BP > 180 mmHg and/or Diastolic BP > 120 mmHg. Requires immediate clinical evaluation and potentially intravenous therapy.

1.3 ATHEROSCLEROSCI CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASE (ASCVD) RISK ASSESSMENT
Prior to initiating pharmacological therapy for Stage 1 Hypertension, a 10-year ASCVD risk assessment should be performed using the Pooled Cohort Equations.
- Low Risk (<10% 10-year ASCVD risk): Manage with lifestyle modifications. Re-evaluate in 3–6 months.
- High Risk (>=10% 10-year ASCVD risk, or history of CVD, diabetes, or chronic kidney disease): Initiate single-agent antihypertensive medication alongside lifestyle modifications.

1.4 SECONDARY HYPERTENSION SCREENING
Secondary hypertension should be suspected in patients with early-onset hypertension (<30 years of age), resistant hypertension (uncontrolled on three medications), or sudden worsening of BP control. Common causes include:
- Renal Artery Stenosis: Suspected in cases of unexplained flash pulmonary edema or significant rise in serum creatinine (>30%) after starting an ACE inhibitor or ARB.
- Primary Aldosteronism: Indicated by persistent hypokalemia combined with hypertension. Screened using the plasma aldosterone-to-renin ratio (ARR).
- Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA): Correlated with nocturnal hypertension and resistant hypertension. Diagnosis confirmed via polysomnography.
- Pheochromocytoma: Characterized by paroxysmal hypertension, headaches, palpitations, and diaphoresis. Screened using fractionated plasma metanephrines.
