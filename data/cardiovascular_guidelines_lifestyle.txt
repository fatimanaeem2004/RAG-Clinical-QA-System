CLINICAL GUIDELINES FOR CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASE AND HYPERTENSION

SECTION 3: LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS AND NON-PHARMACOLOGICAL MANAGEMENT

Lifestyle modifications are recommended for all individuals with elevated blood pressure or hypertension, serving as first-line therapy for low-risk Stage 1 Hypertension and an essential adjunct to pharmacotherapy.

3.1 DIETARY APPROACHES TO STOP HYPERTENSION (DASH) DIET
The DASH diet is highly effective in reducing blood pressure and lowering overall cardiovascular risk.
- Nutritional Profile: Rich in potassium, calcium, magnesium, and dietary fiber. It emphasizes high intake of fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat dairy products. It limits foods high in saturated fat, cholesterol, and refined sugars.
- Impact on Blood Pressure: Consistent adherence to the DASH diet is associated with an average systolic BP reduction of 8 to 11 mmHg in hypertensive individuals.

3.2 SODIUM AND POTASSIUM DIETARY ADJUSTMENTS
- Sodium Restriction: Standard recommendations advise limiting dietary sodium intake to <2,300 mg per day. However, an optimal goal of <1,500 mg per day is recommended for most adults, especially those with hypertension. A reduction of 1,000 mg per day in sodium intake can lower systolic BP by 5 to 6 mmHg.
- Potassium Supplementation: Aim for a dietary potassium intake of 3,500 to 5,000 mg per day, primarily through potassium-rich foods (e.g., bananas, avocados, spinach, sweet potatoes). Increased potassium intake promotes renal sodium excretion and relaxes vascular smooth muscle. Caution: Potassium supplementation is contraindicated in patients with severe chronic kidney disease (CKD) or those taking medications that cause potassium retention (e.g., potassium-sparing diuretics, ACE inhibitors, or ARBs) to avoid life-threatening hyperkalemia.

3.3 PHYSICAL ACTIVITY AND EXERCISE PROTOCOLS
Structured physical activity is a cornerstone of cardiovascular health and blood pressure regulation.
- Aerobic Exercise: Adults should engage in 90 to 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic physical activity per week (e.g., brisk walking, cycling, swimming) or 75 minutes of vigorous activity. This is expected to lower systolic BP by 5 to 8 mmHg.
- Dynamic Resistance Exercise: Performing resistance training (e.g., weight lifting, bodyweight exercises) 2 to 3 times per week provides a BP reduction of approximately 4 mmHg.
- Isometric Resistance Exercise: E.g., handgrip exercises or wall sits, performed 3 times per week, has been shown to reduce blood pressure by 4 to 5 mmHg.

3.4 ALCOHOL MODERATION AND WEIGHT REDUCTION
- Alcohol Consumption: Limit alcohol intake to <=2 standard drinks per day for men, and <=1 standard drink per day for women. One standard drink is defined as 12 ounces of regular beer, 5 ounces of wine, or 1.5 ounces of distilled spirits. Reduction in alcohol intake lowers BP by approximately 4 mmHg.
- Weight Loss: Maintain a healthy body weight (Body Mass Index between 18.5 and 24.9 kg/m2). For individuals who are overweight or obese, weight loss is the most effective lifestyle intervention. Every 1 kg (2.2 lbs) of weight loss is associated with a reduction of approximately 1 mmHg in systolic BP. Achieving a 10 kg weight loss can reduce BP by 5 to 20 mmHg.
- Smoking Cessation: Tobacco smoke causes an acute rise in BP and accelerates atherosclerosis. Smoking cessation does not lead to a chronic reduction in clinic BP but is the single most effective lifestyle change to reduce overall cardiovascular risk.
