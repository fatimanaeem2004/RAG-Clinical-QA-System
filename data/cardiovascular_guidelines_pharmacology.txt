CLINICAL GUIDELINES FOR CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASE AND HYPERTENSION

SECTION 2: PHARMACOLOGICAL MANAGEMENT OF HYPERTENSION

Pharmacotherapy is indicated for Stage 1 Hypertension with high ASCVD risk (>=10%) and all patients with Stage 2 Hypertension. Selecting the appropriate initial drug class depends on patient comorbidities.

2.1 FIRST-LINE ANTIHYPERTENSIVE DRUG CLASSES
Four major drug classes are established as first-line agents:
- Thiazide Diuretics: E.g., Chlorthalidone (preferred due to long half-life and proven trial data) and Hydrochlorothiazide. Mechanism: Inhibit sodium reabsorption in the distal convoluted tubule. Adverse effects: Hypokalemia, hyponatremia, hyperuricemia (may precipitate gout), and hyperglycemia.
- Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme (ACE) Inhibitors: E.g., Lisinopril, Enalapril, Ramipril. Mechanism: Block conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II, reducing vasoconstriction and aldosterone secretion. Highly recommended in patients with diabetes, chronic kidney disease (CKD) with proteinuria, or heart failure. Contraindications: Pregnancy, history of angioedema, and bilateral renal artery stenosis. Side effect: Dry cough (due to bradykinin accumulation).
- Angiotensin II Receptor Blockers (ARBs): E.g., Losartan, Valsartan, Candesartan. Mechanism: Block AT1 receptors. Indicated for patients who require ACE inhibition but develop a dry cough. Similar contraindications to ACE inhibitors; must never be combined with ACE inhibitors.
- Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs): Divided into:
  * Dihydropyridines (e.g., Amlodipine, Nifedipine): Predominantly peripheral vasodilators. Highly effective in elderly patients. Side effect: Dose-dependent peripheral edema.
  * Non-Dihydropyridines (e.g., Diltiazem, Verapamil): Negative inotropes and chronotropes. Commonly used for rate control in atrial fibrillation. Contraindicated in patients with reduced ejection fraction heart failure (HFrEF) due to risk of worsening cardiac output.

2.2 COMORBIDITY-SPECIFIC DRUG SELECTION GUIDELINES
- Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD): ACE inhibitors or ARBs are mandatory first-line therapies for patients with Stage 3 CKD or albuminuria (urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio >= 300 mg/g). They exert renal protective effects by dilating the efferent arteriole, thereby reducing intraglomerular pressure. Monitor creatinine and potassium levels within 1–2 weeks of initiation.
- Diabetes Mellitus: ACE inhibitors or ARBs are first-line choices due to their protection against diabetic nephropathy.
- Heart Failure with Reduced Ejection Fraction (HFrEF): Guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) includes Beta-blockers (specifically Metoprolol Succinate, Carvedilol, or Bisoprolol), ACE inhibitors/ARBs/ARNIs (Sacubitril/Valsartan), Aldosterone Antagonists (Spironolactone), and SGLT2 inhibitors. Note: Beta-blockers are not first-line agents for hypertension unless there is an indication like HFrEF or ischemic heart disease.

2.3 COMBINATION THERAPY STRATEGY
In patients with Stage 2 Hypertension or blood pressure >20/10 mmHg above target, initiate therapy with two first-line agents from different classes.
- Recommended Combinations: ACE inhibitor/ARB paired with a Calcium Channel Blocker, or ACE inhibitor/ARB paired with a Thiazide Diuretic.
- Forbidden Combination: Dual renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) blockade (e.g., combining an ACE inhibitor and an ARB) is strictly contraindicated due to increased risk of hyperkalemia, acute kidney injury, and hypotension.
